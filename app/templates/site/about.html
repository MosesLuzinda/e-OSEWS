Background

Rift Valley fever is a mosquito-borne viral zoonosis that can cause severe disease in humans and devastating reproductive losses in livestock. Outbreaks are often preceded by environmental cues—heavy rainfall, flooding, and vegetation changes—that alter vector breeding sites and human–animal contact patterns. Early detection depends on picking up compatible syndromes in both populations before laboratory confirmation is widely available.

Rift Valley fever in the regional context

RVF is recognised as a priority zoonosis in the Horn of Africa and surrounding areas. Human cases may present as mild febrile illness, retinal disease, or severe haemorrhagic manifestations; in animals, abortion storms and sudden death in young stock are classic signals. Surveillance systems must capture these signals in parallel across community, facility, and veterinary channels, with rapid triage and risk scoring to focus scarce laboratory and outbreak response capacity.

One Health in Isingiro, Rubanda, and Kabale

The three study districts span highland and lower-elevation settings where livestock keeping, crop production, and maternal health services intersect. e-OSEWS operationalises One Health by:

  • Using shared definitions and reporting pathways for syndromes of concern in humans and animals.
  • Linking GPS-located events and environmental observations to maps and alert rules.
  • Supporting joint review by human health, animal health, and district surveillance staff through role-scoped dashboards and validation workflows.
